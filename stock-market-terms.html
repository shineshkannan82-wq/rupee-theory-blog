Stock market glossary

Market language, translated into plain English.

Twenty-four essential terms explained without jargon, plus simple examples that show how each idea works in the real world.

Home/Stock Market Terms

S

Share

One unit of ownership in a company. A company divides its ownership into many shares that investors can hold.

Example: Owning 10 shares out of 1,000 means you own 1% of the company.
S

Stock

A general term for ownership in one or more companies. “Shares” often refers to the specific units owned.

Example: You may invest in stocks and own 20 shares of one company.
E

Equity

The ownership value belonging to shareholders after a company’s liabilities are subtracted from its assets.

Example: Assets of ₹100 crore minus liabilities of ₹40 crore leave ₹60 crore of equity.
M

Market Capitalization

The market value of all outstanding shares: current share price multiplied by total shares.

Example: ₹200 per share × 10 crore shares = ₹2,000 crore market cap.
D

Dividend

A portion of profit a company distributes to shareholders. Companies may also retain profits for growth.

Example: A ₹4 dividend on 100 shares pays the investor ₹400 before tax.
P/E

PE Ratio

Price-to-earnings ratio compares a share’s market price with earnings per share. It indicates how much investors pay per rupee of earnings.

Example: A ₹300 share with ₹15 EPS has a PE ratio of 20.
EPS

EPS

Earnings per share is a company’s profit available to equity shareholders divided by the number of shares.

Example: ₹100 crore profit ÷ 10 crore shares = ₹10 EPS.
ROE

ROE

Return on equity measures how efficiently a company generates profit from shareholder equity.

Example: ₹20 crore profit on ₹100 crore equity equals 20% ROE.
D/E

Debt to Equity Ratio

Compares a company’s debt with shareholder equity. Appropriate levels differ greatly by industry.

Example: ₹50 crore debt ÷ ₹100 crore equity gives a D/E ratio of 0.5.
BV

Book Value

The accounting value of net assets attributable to shareholders. Book value per share divides this by share count.

Example: ₹500 crore net assets ÷ 10 crore shares = ₹50 book value per share.
FV

Face Value

The nominal value assigned to a share by the company. It is used for accounting and some corporate actions, not valuation.

Example: A share may have ₹10 face value while trading at ₹650.
PH

Promoter Holding

The percentage of a company owned by its founders or controlling group. Changes may deserve further investigation.

Example: 55% promoter holding means promoters own 55 of every 100 shares.
FII

FII

Foreign Institutional Investors are overseas institutions investing in Indian markets, such as global funds and pension funds.

Example: A global asset manager buying Indian shares contributes to FII flows.
DII

DII

Domestic Institutional Investors are India-based institutions such as mutual funds, insurers, and pension funds.

Example: An Indian mutual fund buying shares is counted as DII activity.
↑

Bull Market

A broad period of rising prices and investor optimism. There is no guarantee that all shares rise.

Example: A major index gaining steadily over many months may be called a bull market.
↓

Bear Market

A sustained period of falling market prices and weak sentiment, commonly associated with a 20% decline from a peak.

Example: An index falling from 20,000 to 16,000 is down 20%.
IPO

IPO

An Initial Public Offering is the first sale of a company’s shares to public investors before listing on an exchange.

Example: Investors apply during the offer; allotted shares list later on the exchange.
B

Bonus Share

Additional shares issued to existing shareholders without direct payment, in a stated ratio. Total value does not instantly multiply.

Example: A 1:1 bonus gives one new share for each share already held.
SS

Stock Split

A company divides each share into more shares with a proportionately lower face value and market price.

Example: In a 1:5 split, one ₹500 share becomes five shares near ₹100 each.
C

CAGR

Compound Annual Growth Rate is the smooth annualized rate that connects a starting value to an ending value over time.

Example: ₹1 lakh becoming about ₹2 lakh in 10 years is roughly 7.2% CAGR.
52H

52 Week High

The highest price at which a security traded during the previous 52 weeks. It is a reference point, not a valuation signal.

Example: A stock at ₹480 with a 52-week high of ₹500 is 4% below that high.
52L

52 Week Low

The lowest trading price during the previous 52 weeks. A low price alone does not make a stock inexpensive.

Example: Poor business results can justify a price near its 52-week low.
V

Volume

The number of shares traded during a period. Unusually high volume can signal increased market interest.

Example: If 8 lakh shares change hands today, daily volume is 8 lakh.
L

Liquidity

How easily an investment can be bought or sold near its current price without causing a major price change.

Example: A heavily traded large company is usually more liquid than a rarely traded small one.
